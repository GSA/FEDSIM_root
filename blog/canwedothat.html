by Chris Hamm

Can We Do It?

July 15, 2018

This blog will be a mix of big “I” and little “i” innovation—we will share posts about how FEDSIM is shaping Federal acquisition, both with ideas we are testing and activities already in practice.

In biology, we learn about homeostasis—the tendency for systems to reach equilibrium. In economics, we learn that people respond to incentives. In psychology, we learn that individuals act in ways that are not logical due to variety of biases. Mix all that together and you get humans and organizations that act in ways that are not optimal.

In government organizations, there is always a tendency toward avoiding risk—that’s how the incentives align. Bureaucracies follow a predictable cycle, gravitating away from supporting the mission until an external factor changes the environment (e.g., an election, personnel changes, budget changes, oversight, public failure, media coverage).

This long preamble serves as context for explaining why acquisition in the Federal Government is often mocked for bad deals (e.g., $600 hammer), ethical lapses, and an operational tempo that sloths would envy. Industry, political leaders, and the public demand innovation and change. Why is it so hard?

I’ve worked at GSA for 19 years—a really interesting place, but still... 19 years! I know lots of people like me. Well-meaning public servants who balance conflicting policies and shifting priorities. One thing that that we are really good at is compliance—following rules. Following the rules and established process and documenting procedures is the recipe for long-term safety in a career. It is also a great formula for disillusionment, stagnation, and losing a little bit of your soul every day. In the movie Jerry Maguire, Tom Cruise’s character finally reaches his limit and pens a heartfelt memo on how to change his organization. He emails it to everyone, and then he gets fired. That’s not how to change an organization, particularly something that sits at the intersection of billions of dollars of taxpayer money.

Instead, it starts from the top, with leadership and support. Public servants will only increase their risk posture with active support from leaders, particularly those who take the long view. In 2016, the Office of Management and Budget (OMB) put out a call for Federal Agencies to name Acquisition Innovation Advocates (AIAs). Somehow, my name popped out of the sorting hat. We (the AIAs) have monthly meetings, during which each agency shares their innovative activities. My takeaways: NASA is awesome, DoD worries about global competitiveness, DHS has a great program, and they all started with a combination and leadership and special authorities. Now, GSA has a new authority and leadership support to start trying new things. GSA’s Assisted Acquisition Services (AAS) will be piloting a variety of new concepts over the next year, such as:

  • Small Business Innovation and Research (SBIR) Phase III
  • Commercial Solutions Openings (CSO)
  • Expanded Debriefings

Instead, it starts from the top, with leadership and support. Public servants will only increase their risk posture with active support from leaders, particularly those who take the long view.

This blog will be a mix of big “I” and little “i” innovation—we will share posts about how FEDSIM is shaping Federal acquisition, both with ideas we are testing and activities already in practice. One thing that I learned from having kids and going to AIA meetings is the importance of sharing and granting permission to take risks to build new capability.
